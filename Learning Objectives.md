# **Knowledge Unit 1 \- Disability Awareness**

## **Every computing student should know this, regardless of their specialization**

- **Ableism and Etiquette**: Students should be familiar with the “Nothing About Us Without Us” movement, specifically, that disabled people face systemic biases. Understand etiquette for interacting with people with disabilities.

- **Basic Categories of Disabilities**: Students should be able to list broad categories of disability, including: vision, auditory, motor, and cognitive. Students should understand that disability can be permanent, temporary, or situational, and that disability is a varied and nuanced experience that extends beyond these basic classifications.

- **Ethics:** Students should be able to apply ethical frameworks such as the principles outlined in the ACM Code of Ethics and Professional Conduct to assess the ethical implications of technology for people with disabilities.

- **Assistive Technology Awareness:** Students should be able to demonstrate awareness of some assistive technology like screen readers, switch devices, captions, voice commands.

- **History of Accessibility**: Students should be familiar with disabled people’s roles in the evolution of the telephone, text messaging, and other technical systems. Students should understand how disability advocacy and activism contributed to passing accessibility legislation.

- **Make a Case for Accessibility**: Students should be able to make a pitch for why accessibility is important. They should be able to draw from human, legal, business, and software inaccessibility prevalence information.

- **Motivations for Accessibility**: Students should understand key motivations for why accessibility matters, including its human impact, civil rights and legal implications, and effects on software quality and development costs. Students should recognize that accessibility serves a large and diverse population with permanent, temporary, and situational access needs. Students should be aware of common structural reasons why accessibility gaps persist in software development and understand the consequences of these gaps.

- **Use Common Assistive Technology**: Students should be able to perform basic functions using some assistive technology such as screen readers, switch access, digital magnifier, and keyboard-only navigation.

## **Important for computing programs that want to strengthen their accessibility coverage**

- **Basic Models of Disability:** Students should understand that there are different models of disability, and be able to define key ones, e.g., the medical model (which views disability as a problem in the person) vs. the social model (which views disability as a contextual problem that is disabling).

- **Disability Identities**: Students should understand that disability identity, experience, and expression vary across contexts, including non apparent, chronic, and episodic disabilities. Students should recognize that language preferences vary within disabled communities (for example, identity first vs person first), and practice respectful communication by using participant or community preferred terminology.

# **Knowledge Unit 2 \- Accessibility Design**

## **Every computing student should know this, regardless of their specialization**

- **Design Accessible Software**: Students should be able to apply web and mobile accessibility guidelines to make new designs accessible, and update legacy designs. They should be able to design software that can be customized. Students should be able to distinguish between different design approaches, including the Principles of Universal Design and how they may be used for different kinds of disabilities, and they should be able to apply industry accessibility guidelines, (e.g., from Google and Microsoft).

- **Design Guidelines and Knowledge**: Students should be able to describe users with disabilities using proper language. Apply W3C accessibility guidelines to design. They should be able to gain knowledge of Web Content Accessibility Guidelines, what it means to be compliant, and how to meet compliance. Students should become familiar with specific details such as how to choose appropriate colors, layout design, and font selection, and know how to use design tool annotations.

- **Methods and Approaches**: Students should be familiar with design methods and approaches that include accessibility. They should understand how to collect software requirements from users with disabilities, e.g., ask deaf participants if they prefer a captionist or an interpreter prior to conducting usability studies.

- **Design for Assistive Technology Users**: Students should be able to design technologies that work seamlessly with assistive technologies so that people using assistive technologies can effectively complete tasks.




## **Important for computing programs that want to strengthen their accessibility coverage**

- **Special Topics**: Students should be able design for interoperability between accessible and assistive technology so that people using assistive technologies can effectively complete tasks.

# **Knowledge Unit 3 \- Accessibility Implementation**

## **Every computing student should know this, regardless of their specialization**

- **Accessible Rich Internet Applications (ARIA)**: Students should understand the purpose of Accessible Rich Internet Applications (ARIA) and explain the role of ARIA attributes in enhancing the accessibility of web content. Students should be able to identify common ARIA roles such as roles for buttons, links, landmarks, and attributes like aria-label, aria-labelled by, and aria-described by. Students should understand when ARIA is appropriate to use and when it should be avoided, recognizing that incorrect or unnecessary use can create accessibility barriers rather than remove them.

- **Accessibility Guidelines**: Students should be able to apply accessibility guidelines (such as WCAG) to develop software. For example, implement keyboard navigation so that contents are accessed in a logical order.

- **Accessibility Requirements**: Students should be able to identify accessibility requirements from user needs and translate them into clear, testable requirements and acceptance criteria.

- **Software Architecture**: Students should understand the impact of software architecture on accessibility. They should be able to evaluate and select frameworks and libraries that support accessibility features and conform to accessibility standards.

- **Assistive Technology Documentation**: Students should know about an assistive technology to be able to create documentation detailing the components and functionalities of that assistive technology.

## **Important for computing programs that want to strengthen their accessibility coverage**

- **Accessibility APIs**: Students should be able to demonstrate working knowledge of and the ability to use accessibility APIs, such as the Apple Accessibility API, Java Accessibility API, and Microsoft UI Automation.

- **Assistive Technology Architecture**: Students should understand the overall architecture of one example of assistive technology. They should understand how an assistive technology, e.g., a screen reader, interfaces with operating systems and web browsers.

# **Knowledge Unit 4 \- Accessibility Evaluation**

## **Every computing student should know this, regardless of their specialization**

- **Automated Accessibility Testing**: Students should recognize and use automated accessibility testing tools, such as the open source accessibility testing engine Axe and the web evaluation suite WAVE.

- **Testing Reports**: Students should be able to interpret automated accessibility testing reports. They should understand the potential impact of issues on users, and document needed improvements based on the test results.

## **Important for computing programs that want to strengthen their accessibility coverage**

- **Accessibility Testing Integration**: Students should be able to integrate automated accessibility tests into continuous integration (CI) and continuous deployment (CD) pipelines.

- **Testing with Assistive Technology**: Students should be able to evaluate software accessibility by using an assistive technology, e.g. Screen reader and a keyboard for navigation.

- **Test Planning**: Students should be able to create a test plan that compliments automated testing tools with other testing approaches, and that evaluates the software for users with different types of disabilities, e.g., testing plan for blind users, or for users with motor disabilities.

- **Usability Testing**: Students should be able to define usability testing and explain its significance in evaluating software accessibility from the perspective of individuals with disabilities. They should be able to specify techniques for collecting feedback from users with disabilities during testing, including surveys and interviews.

- **Test Results**: Students should be able to prioritize and remediate detected accessibility issues. They should triage issues based on severity, user impact, and task importance, using clear criteria such as whether issues are blockers, compliance failures, or usability concerns. Students should implement fixes and verify remediation through appropriate retesting, including automated, manual, and assistive technology testing when relevant.

- **User Tasks**: Students should be able to design user tasks that represent the typical interactions users perform using assistive technology such as switch devices or voice recognition.

- **Testing Tools**: Students should compare automated testing tools and understand their limitations. They should be able to set up and configure accessibility testing tools (for example, installing scanners or linters, configuring rules and thresholds, and generating reports) to assess websites and applications. Students should understand what automated tools can and cannot validate and when manual and assistive technology testing is required.

# **Knowledge Unit 5 \- Accessibility Profession and Continuous Learning**

## **Every computing student should know this, regardless of their specialization**

- **Continuous Learning**: Students should be familiar with accessibility academic and professional resources and communities to stay informed about evolving accessibility standards and tools.

- **Professional Accessibility Skills**: Students should understand the accessibility skills expected in general technology roles. They should be able to recognize the need to work collaboratively with accessibility experts and people with disabilities to ensure accessible technology.

## **Important for computing programs that want to strengthen their accessibility coverage**

- **Accessibility Specialization**: Students should clearly define how the accessibility specialist’s role differs from that of general designers, developers, and testers in terms of accessibility knowledge and skills.

- **Awareness of Accessibility Certification**: Students should be aware of accessibility professional certifications and understand when certified or specialized expertise may be required.

- **Identifying Accessibility Barriers in  Emerging Technologies**: Students should be able to identify the accessibility implications of emerging technologies. They should be able to recognize the unique accessibility challenges that emerging technologies pose for disabled people.
