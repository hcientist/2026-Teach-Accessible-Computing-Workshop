## Instructor Self-Audit Questions

Recommendations for reflexive practice:
* Intervention design: 
    * Does my activity assume all participants are non-disabled?
* Language: 
    * Have I used deficit language (e.g., struggling groups) in my materials?
* Framing: 
    * Does my activity frame accessibility as a legal checkbox or a human right?
* Inclusion: 
    * Is any disabled person involved in this activity as a contributor?
* Evaluation instrument: 
    * Does my question allow students to express assumptions or only report facts?

## Case Study: How Inclusive is This Assignment?

Design a mobile app feature to help blind users who find the UX frustrating (or otherwise problematic) when finding directions on a map.
First, understand the access problem. Turn on the screen reader and find directions between two locations on Google Maps.
Second, create a design concept that makes map navigation easy to use for blind people. Turn off the screen when testing your design concept.
Last, write a report on the usability and accessibility of the mobile app feature. On a scale from 1 to 5, where 1 is strongly disagree and 5 is strongly agree, rate the following statements:
I have more empathy for blind users.
In my future projects, I will design with accessibility in mind.


### Response

1. students may not be ready to just turn on the screen reader (and once they have, that they may have difficulty with the map app may have little relation to problems the interface creates for blind people)
2. we didn't add context about turning on the screen reader (and turning off the screen) not being equal to being blind
3. it's a bit unclear how the students can design something that is easy for blind people unless they're blind or have previous training/experience
4. the evaluation feels rather value-laden (would i be comfortable to says i don't have more empathy? or that i won't design with a11y?)
5. perhaps some scaffolding toward research (what metaphors or patterns are used and what else might have been used to be better)
6. simulations may not be helpful for designing (perhaps more for understanding/intro and maybe for evaluating, to respect and protect testers' time)
7. ask, "who is being excluded in this case"?
8. there's a difference between "sympathy" and "empathy"

## Day 1 Takeaways

1. Accessibility is a fundamental computing competency. It prepares students to design technologies that are usable by people with diverse abilities and is increasingly expected by industry.
2. Accessibility goes beyond compliance. It improves usability, supports inclusion, and benefits a broad range of users.
3. Assistive technology and accessibility work together. Accessible software enables assistive technologies to function effectively.
4. Accessibility can be taught throughout the software development lifecycle. Today's learning objectives illustrate opportunities to integrate accessibility into design, implementation, testing, and professional practice; not just a single lecture or course.
5. Industry expects graduates to understand accessibility. The skills gap continues to grow, creating opportunities for educators to better prepare future computing professionals.
6. People with disabilities should be included throughout the design process. The perspectives shared today reinforce that accessibility is most effective when informed by lived experience.

#### From KA1 Disability Awareness:

* Basic Categories of Disabilities: Students should be able to list broad categories of disability, including: vision, auditory, motor, and cognitive. Students should understand that disability can be permanent, temporary, or situational, and that disability is a varied and nuanced experience that extends beyond these basic classifications.
* Ethics: Students should be able to apply ethical frameworks such as the principles outlined in the ACM Code of Ethics and Professional Conduct to assess the ethical implications of technology for people with disabilities.
* Assistive Technology Awareness: Students should be able to demonstrate awareness of some assistive technology like screen readers, switch devices, captions, voice commands.
